Reading the Moon

An interactive exhibit where you explore and identify the Moon's four major rock-forming minerals under a polarizing microscope, label your own thin-section dataset, contribute to a growing thin section dataset for a foundation model of the Moon, and observe how a self-improving AI agent performing per-grain segmentation in real time.

Intro

Lunar rocks are simple.

The Moon has no liquid water and no plate tectonics, so its minerals never weathered into clays or micas. A lunar thin section is a clean mosaic of just a handful of primary minerals, formed billions of years ago and frozen in place.

Two kinds of rock dominate. The bright highlands are anorthosite, almost pure plagioclase feldspar. The dark maria are basalt, built from pyroxene, plagioclase, olivine, and titanium-rich ilmenite. Learn those four and you can read most of the lunar surface.

A 30-micron-thick slice of rock is placed between two crossed polarizing filters. Each mineral splits light in its own way, glowing in interference colors. Rotate the stage and grains brighten, shift, and snap to black at extinction. The pattern of relief, cleavage, twinning, and color is a fingerprint.
